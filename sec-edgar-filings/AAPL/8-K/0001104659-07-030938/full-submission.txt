<p style="margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;"><b><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">Item 8.01&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160;&#160; Other Events.</font></b></p>

<p style="margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;">On Tuesday, April 24,
2007, the United States Securities and Exchange Commission (the &#147;Commission&#148;)
issued a press release announcing that the Commission has filed a civil action
against two former officers of Apple Inc. (&#147;Apple&#148;) in connection with past
stock option grants.&#160; According to the
press release, in connection with the filing of the complaints, the Commission
announced that it would not bring an enforcement action against Apple. &#160;In its press release, the Commission stated: &#147;The
Commission also announced today that it would not bring any enforcement action
against Apple based in part on its swift, extensive, and extraordinary
cooperation in the Commission&#146;s investigation. Apple&#146;s cooperation consisted
of, among other things, prompt self-reporting, an independent internal
investigation, the sharing of the results of that investigation with the
government, and the implementation of new controls designed to prevent the
recurrence of fraudulent conduct.&#148;</font></p>

<p style="margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;">The full text of the
press release issued by the Commission is attached hereto as Exhibit 99.1 and

<p align="right" style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-align:right;text-autospace:none;"><b><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">Exhibit 99.1</font></b></p>

<p style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><b><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">SEC CHARGES FORMER APPLE GENERAL COUNSEL FOR ILLEGAL STOCK OPTION
BACKDATING</font></b></p>

<p style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><b><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">Commission Also Settles Claims Against Former Apple CFO for $3.5 Million</font></b></p>

<p style="color:black;font-size:12.0pt;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><b><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE</font></b><font face="Times New Roman"><br>
</font><b><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">2007-70</font></b></p>

<p style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;">Washington, D.C., April 24,
2007 - The Securities and Exchange Commission today filed charges against two
former senior executives of Apple, Inc. in a matter involving improper stock
option backdating. The Commission accused former General Counsel Nancy R.
Heinen of participating in the fraudulent backdating of options granted to
Apple&#146;s top officers that caused the company to underreport its expenses by
nearly $40 million. The Commission&#146;s complaint alleges that Heinen, of Portola
Valley, Calif., caused Apple to backdate two large options grants to senior
executives of Apple &#151; a February 2001 grant of 4.8 million options to Apple&#146;s
Executive Team and a December 2001 grant of 7.5 million options to Apple Chief
Executive Officer Steve Jobs &#151; and altered company records to conceal the
fraud.</font></p>

<p style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;">The Commission also filed,
and simultaneously settled, charges against former Apple Chief Financial
Officer Fred D. Anderson, of Atherton, Calif., alleging that Anderson should
have noticed Heinen&#146;s efforts to backdate the Executive Team grant but failed
to take steps to ensure that Apple&#146;s financial statements were correct. As part
of the settlement, Anderson agreed (without admitting or denying the
allegations) to pay approximately $3.5 million in disgorgement and penalties.</font></p>

<p style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;">Linda Chatman Thomsen,
Director of the SEC&#146;s Division of Enforcement, stated, &#147;The Apple case
demonstrates the Commission&#146;s ongoing commitment to take action against stock
options backdating and other executive compensation abuses. When corporate
officers enrich themselves at the expense of a company&#146;s shareholders, the
Commission will hold the responsible individuals accountable, particularly
where, as here, the responsible individuals are among those obligated to ensure
that the company complies with all applicable securities laws and that its
financial statements are accurate.&#148;</font></p>

<p style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;">Marc J. Fagel, Associate
Regional Director of the SEC&#146;s San Francisco Regional Office, stated, &#147;Apple&#146;s
shareholders relied on Heinen and Anderson, as respected legal and accounting
professionals, to ensure the accurate reporting of the company&#146;s executive
compensation. Instead, they failed in their duties as gatekeepers and caused
Apple to conceal millions of dollars in stock option expenses.&#148;</font></p>

<p style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;">According to the Commission&#146;s
complaint, filed in the Northern District of California, Apple granted 4.8
million options to six members of its executive team (including Heinen and
Anderson) in February 2001. Because the options were in-the-money when granted
(i.e. could be exercised to purchase Apple shares at a below market price),
Apple was required to report a compensation charge in its publicly filed
financial statements. The Commission alleges that, in order to avoid reporting
this expense, Heinen caused Apple to backdate options to January 17, 2001, when
Apple&#146;s share price was substantially lower. Heinen is also alleged to have
directed her staff to prepare documents falsely indicating that Apple&#146;s Board
had approved the Executive Team grant on January 17. As a result, Apple failed
to record approximately $18.9 million in compensation expenses associated with
the option grant. Anderson, who should have realized the implications of Heinen&#146;s
actions, failed to disclose key information to Apple&#146;s auditors and neglected
to ensure that the company&#146;s financial statements were accurate. Both Heinen
and Anderson personally received millions of dollars in unreported compensation
as a result of the backdating.</font></p>

<p style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;">The Commission&#146;s complaint
also alleges improprieties in connection with a December 2001 grant of 7.5
million options to CEO Steve Jobs. Although the options were in-the-money at
that time, Heinen &#151; as with</font></p>

<p style="margin:0pt 0pt .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;">&nbsp;</font></p>

<p style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;">the Executive Team grant &#151;
caused Apple to backdate the grant to October 19, 2001, when Apple&#146;s share
price was lower. As a result, the Commission alleges that Heinen caused Apple
to improperly fail to record $20.3 million in compensation expense associated
with the in-the-money options grant. The Commission further alleges that Heinen
then signed fictitious Board minutes stating that the Board had approved the
grant to Jobs on October 19 at a &#147;Special Meeting of the Board of Directors&#148; &#151;
a meeting that, in fact, never occurred.</font></p>

<p style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;">Heinen is charged with,
among other things, violating the antifraud provisions of the Securities Act of
1933 and the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, lying to Apple&#146;s auditors, and
violating prohibitions on circumventing internal controls. The Commission is
seeking injunctive relief, disgorgement, and money penalties against Heinen, in
addition to an order barring her from serving as an officer or director of a
public company.</font></p>

<p style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;">Anderson, without admitting
or denying the allegations in the Commission&#146;s complaint, has agreed to a
permanent injunction from further violations of the antifraud, reporting,
internal controls, and other provisions of the federal securities laws. Anderson
also will disgorge approximately $3.49 million in ill-gotten gains and
prejudgment interest, representing the in-the-money portion of the Executive
Team options grant that Anderson exercised, and will pay a civil penalty of
$150,000.</font></p>

<p style="color:black;line-height:normal;margin:0pt 0pt 12.0pt;text-autospace:none;"><font size="2" color="black" face="Times New Roman" style="color:windowtext;font-size:10.0pt;">The Commission also announced
today that it would not bring any enforcement action against Apple based in
part on its swift, extensive, and extraordinary cooperation in the Commission&#146;s
investigation. Apple&#146;s cooperation consisted of, among other things, prompt
self-reporting, an independent internal investigation, the sharing of the
results of that investigation with the government, and the implementation of
new controls designed to prevent the recurrence of fraudulent conduct.</font></p>
